Vegas Guide

Everything you need to know before you go.

Las Vegas is unlike anywhere else. It’s a city that runs on 24-hour energy, midweek parties, high-end dining, five-minute weddings, and enough shows, hotels, and chaos to overwhelm just about anyone. But with the right prep, Las Vegas is one of the easiest — and most entertaining — cities to visit.

This guide covers everything you need to know before you land: from transport to tipping, weather to what to pack, and how to do Vegas right without losing time, money, or your mind.

When to Visit Las Vegas

Las Vegas is open year-round, but some months are better than others.

Best months:

– March to May

– October to early November

Why: Pool weather is ideal, crowds are manageable, and hotel rates are lower than summer.

Avoid: July and August if you hate the heat. It hits 110°F and barely cools at night. December is great for deals, but pools are closed and some shows go dark.

Where to Stay

– March to May

– On the Strip: Best for first-timers and short trips. Everything is walkable (sort of).

– Downtown (Fremont Street): Cheaper, louder, more local. Great for bar crawls and casino energy.

– Off-Strip: Calmer. Good for business travel, long stays, or trips where you don’t want to be surrounded by tourists 24/7.

Need help deciding? Check our [Where to Stay] page for hotel breakdowns by trip type.

Getting Around

You don’t need a car. Seriously. Parking is expensive, traffic is constant, and walking + rideshare covers almost everything.

– Walking: The Strip is long. Blocks are bigger than they look. Wear good shoes.

– Lyft/Uber: Reliable and cheaper than taxis.

– Monorail: Covers major Strip stops (especially east-side resorts).

– Free Trams: Operate between a few hotels (like Bellagio–Park MGM–Aria).

– Buses: The Deuce runs the Strip 24/7. Affordable but slow.

What to Pack

Vegas is extreme — air conditioning inside, blazing heat outside. Think versatility.

– Comfortable shoes (you’ll walk more than you think)

– Light layers

– Swimwear

– A “night out” outfit (clubs enforce dress codes)

– Portable charger (Strip signage kills your battery)

– Sunscreen — even in winte

– ID (you’ll get carded everywhere, even at 40)

Money + Budget Tips

Vegas can go from affordable to expensive fast. Here’s how to keep it under control:

– Use ATMs inside casinos — they’re cheaper than convenience store ones

– Always ask for split checks before you order

– Happy hours are everywhere, especially off the Strip

– Buffets are worth it if you eat big — skip if you’re light

– Casinos offer free drinks while you gamble — tip the server and drink slowly

– Resort fees aren’t included in base hotel prices — budget $35–$50 extra per night

Tipping in Las Vegas

Tipping isn’t optional — it’s expected.

– Bartenders: $1–$2 per drink

– Valet: $3–$5

– Housekeeping: $3–$5/day

– Dealers: $5–$10 per session if you’re winning

– Bellhops: $2–$3 per bag

Tip well and you’ll get better service, faster drinks, and friendlier everything.

Vegas Do’s and Don’ts

Do:

– Book dinner and shows in advance

– Carry cash for tips, games, and emergencies

– Stay hydrated — desert heat is sneaky

– Take breaks. The Strip will still be there after your nap.

Don’t:

– Walk barefoot in your hotel room (trust us)

– Try to “wing it” on club entry — get on a guest list or book ahead

– Assume you’ll win — treat gambling as part of the entertainment

– Stay too far from where you plan to spend your time

Best Free Things to Do in Vegas

Vegas can be surprisingly affordable if you know where to look.

– Bellagio Fountain Show

– Fremont Street light show

– The Volcano at The Mirage (still active — check times)

– Conservatory & Botanical Gardens at Bellagio

– Art displays at Aria and The Shops at Crystals

– People-watching (it’s a sport in this city)

A Few Final Tips

– Most people underestimate the heat, the walking, and the cost

– There’s more to Vegas than the Strip — explore Fremont, Chinatown, and local restaurants

– Drink water, eat something before clubbing, and don’t overbook your schedule

– Check your hotel map before you arrive — some lobbies are a 10-minute walk from the street
